Production 2: New Literacies Reflection

       

Brayboy and Maughan’s (2008) Indigenous Knowledges and the Story of the Bean challenges dominant educational paradigms by showing how Knowledges are lived, reciprocal, and vital to cultural survival. In Indigenous contexts, Knowledges are inseparable from responsibility and survival, while Western schooling often reduces learning to abstract, fragmented facts. Knowledge is not a possession but a practice enacted through relationships and ethical commitments.

The Story of the Bean illustrates that learning is tied to cosmology, land, and collective responsibility. Teachers are not distant authorities but accountable community members. This may involve co-teaching with Elders, leading projects rooted in local ecosystems, or grounding lessons in community histories. Education flourishes through responsibility and care—not detachment.

Reframing curriculum and assessment through this lens emphasizes reflection, engagement, and accountability rather than memorization. Outdoor learning—survival skills, canoe journeys, or traditional navigation—deepens relationships with the land and community. Success is measured less by standardized testing than by stewardship, skill, and reciprocal responsibility. Standardized assessments often undermine these goals by prioritizing individual achievement over relational approaches.

Relationality also extends into digital environments. Initiatives like He Ao Hou: A New World (Kanaeokana, 2020) and Indigenous-led VR projects (CBC, 2018) immerse learners in ancestral languages and stories while respecting cultural protocols. For example, one Hawaiian project requires ceremony before accessing digital content, ensuring technology mediates rather than flattens relationships. Similarly, language apps and community archives show how digital tools sustain kinship networks and collective memory. Innovation here matters less for novelty than for reinforcing cultural survival and continuity.

Such approaches open new horizons in technology education. Indigenous learners are designing VR storytelling platforms, language apps, and 3D-printed cultural artifacts. Some communities use 3D printing to recreate lost treasures, reconnecting youth with tradition while teaching design and engineering. Even beyond community contexts, these tools remain grounded in protocol, showing that technical skill and cultural preservation are inseparable.

Barriers, however, are significant. Indigenous Knowledges are plural and diverse, not reducible to a single story (Levac et al., 2018; ABC Life Literacy Canada, 2024). Yet systemic underfunding, standardized testing, limited Indigenous leadership, and restricted digital access continue to marginalize them (People for Education, 2016; Sosyalarastirmalar, 2025). These are not neutral oversights but institutional decisions about which Knowledges count.

A path forward requires epistemic pluralism—valuing multiple knowledge systems as equal rather than hierarchical. This involves investing in Indigenous-led programs, expanding digital infrastructure in remote communities, and grounding curricula in community and land. The First Nations Technology Council in British Columbia provides one model by integrating coding workshops with Indigenous governance. Mentorship by Indigenous professionals, alongside teacher preparation in culturally sustaining pedagogy, builds the trust needed to make education accountable to community. Sustained funding and Indigenous leadership are essential.

Brayboy and Maughan (2008) remind us that Indigenous Knowledges are ethical and political, rooted in survival and sovereignty. Supporting them affirms Indigenous identities and reorients education toward reciprocity, accountability, and justice. For educators, this means centering responsibility to community. For policymakers, it means resourcing Indigenous leadership and ensuring equitable access to technology. Ultimately, Indigenous Knowledges do not simply add content to existing systems—they transform education by reshaping relationships among learners, communities, and the land. When embraced as plural, sovereign, and relational, they reveal knowledge as a living practice vital to sustaining just and culturally rooted futures.
